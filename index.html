History Of Bangli

According to the Kehen Temple Inscription stored in the Kehen Temple, it is told that in the 11th century Bangli Village developed an epidemic called dryness which caused many residents to die. The other residents who were still alive and well were scared to death, so they flocked to leave the village to avoid the plague. As a result, Bangli Village became empty because no one dared to live there. Raja Ida Bhatara Guru Sri Adikunti Ketana who was reigning at that time tried to overcome the plague. After the situation recovered, the king

ordered his sons and daughters named Dhana Dewi Ketu to invite residents back to Bangli Village to jointly build and repai r their respective houses as well as to hold ceremonies / yadnya in Kasa, Karo, Katiga, Kapat, Kalima, Kalima, Kanem, Kapitu, Kaulu, Kasanga, Kadasa, Yjahstha and Sadha . In addition, the king also ordered all residents to increase their descendants in the Pura Loka Serana area in Bangli Village and allowed clearing the forest to make rice fields and water channels. For this reason, at every major ceremony, the people in Bangli Village have to pray.

Traditions of Bangli

Traditions of Bangli

Bangli Regency has a number of cultural diversity which is a combination that is the core of the acculturation and tolerance of the people who inhabit this pluralistic region. The acculturation of Bangli cultural diversity is in the form of acculturation between Balinese-Javanese, Balinese-Chinese, Hindu-Islamic, Hindu-Buddhist, and Hindu-Christian cultures. There are several traditions that are commonly practiced by the Bangli people. including: pemakaman di trunyan, perang papah, Ngusaba Tegen.

Perang Papah

There is a culture of the Babuang dance / Papah war in the Pengotan Traditional Village, Bangli District, Bangli Regency which is still being carried out today, the Babuang dance activities that were followed by Jero first, the traditional village people who have sat in Bale Agung and the seka teruna who participated in this Babuang dance, all who participate in this dance wear only kamben.

Ngusaba Tegen

Ngusaba Tegen is a series of Hindu religious ceremonies that are held once a year, which is a unique tradition because the ritual is only held in the village of Kedisan Kintamani, which is why this tradition has become something different and becomes its own attraction for other residents in Bali, even for tourists. This is a routine annual attraction that is quite interesting to witness, especially since Kintamani itself is a popular tourist destination in Bali, so the Ngusaba Tegen tradition which is held in Kedisan village will add to the offer of tourist attractions that Kintamani has.

Pemakaman di Trunyan

This traditional village of Trunyan in Bangli is one of the Balinese Aga villages, which are indigenous Balinese who were not influenced by outside culture when Majapahit rule in Bali, still upholding various cultural heritage of their ancestors. For this reason, the traditional village of Trunyan has a number of unique traditions including funerals or burials. The Balinese people themselves are Hindu in their daily lives, every person who dies will have a Ngaben ceremony either by burying the body or directly cremating it.
